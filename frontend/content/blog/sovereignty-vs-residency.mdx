## The Residency Illusion

When a hyperscaler advertises a "Canada Region," most buyers assume their data is under Canadian law. It isn't.

AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure are all US-headquartered companies. Under the **Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act (CLOUD Act)**, enacted in 2018, US law enforcement can compel any US-controlled company to produce data — regardless of where that data is physically stored.

This means data sitting in `ca-central-1` can be accessed by a US court without Canadian judicial oversight.

## What True Sovereignty Looks Like

True data sovereignty requires three things:

1. **Canadian-incorporated operating entity** — not a subsidiary of a foreign parent.
2. **Canadian jurisdiction** — Canadian courts are the sole legal authority over the data.
3. **No foreign compulsion** — no foreign government can issue a legally binding production order.

Xcelsior meets all three. We're incorporated in Canada, with no US parent company, and our infrastructure is operated exclusively by Canadian entities.

## Why This Matters for AI

Canada's AI ecosystem is growing fast. The federal government has committed $2.4 billion to AI infrastructure, and the $300M AI Compute Access Fund requires compute to be on "Canadian infrastructure" for the maximum 67% rebate.

But "Canadian infrastructure" operated by a US parent doesn't qualify for the highest rebate tier — and more importantly, it doesn't protect your training data, model weights, or research outputs from foreign disclosure orders.

## The Bottom Line

Data residency is a geography question. Data sovereignty is a **legal** question. If your compute provider has a US parent, your data is subject to US law — no matter where the servers sit.
