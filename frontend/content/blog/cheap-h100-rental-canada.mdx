## The H100 Problem

NVIDIA's H100 is the most sought-after GPU for AI training. With 80GB of HBM3 memory and massive tensor core throughput, it's the chip that powers GPT-scale model training. The problem? It's expensive:

- **Buying outright**: $30,000–$40,000 per card (if you can find one)
- **AWS (p5.48xlarge, 8x H100)**: ~$98/hr USD — that's ~$135 CAD/hr
- **Reserved pricing**: Requires 1–3 year commits at $50,000+ USD

For Canadian teams, the math gets worse once you factor in USD/CAD exchange rates, cross-border data transfer costs, and CLOUD Act compliance risk.

### 2. Optimize Your Training

Before throwing more H100 hours at the problem:

- **Mixed precision training** — Use FP16/BF16 to nearly double throughput
- **Gradient checkpointing** — Trade compute for VRAM, use fewer GPUs
- **Efficient data loading** — Don't let your H100s sit idle waiting for data
- **Checkpointing** — Xcelsior supports automatic checkpointing so interrupted jobs resume, not restart

### 3. Consider Multi-GPU vs. Single-GPU

An 8x H100 node is great for large-scale training, but many workloads can be broken into smaller parallel jobs on fewer GPUs. Running 4 separate 1x H100 jobs on Xcelsior may be cheaper than a single 4x H100 reserved instance on AWS.

### 4. Use RTX 4090s for Development

H100s are for production training runs. For prototyping, hyperparameter sweeps, and small-scale experiments, an RTX 4090 at ~$0.80 CAD/hr on Xcelsior gives you 24GB VRAM at a fraction of the cost.

## When to Use an H100

H100s make sense for:

- **Large language model training** (7B+ parameters)
- **Multi-node distributed training** with fast NVLink/InfiniBand
- **Production inference** serving at scale
- **Large batch sizes** that benefit from 80GB HBM3

For fine-tuning, LoRA, inference on smaller models, or development work, A100s ($3.20 CAD/hr) or RTX 4090s ($0.80 CAD/hr) often deliver better cost-efficiency.
